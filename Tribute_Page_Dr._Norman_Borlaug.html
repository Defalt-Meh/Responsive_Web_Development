Dr. Norman Borlaug

Dr. Norman Borlaug in his field
Dr. Norman Borlaug was an American agronomist who won the Nobel Peace Prize for his work in developing high-yielding varieties of cereal grains.

Timeline of Dr. Borlaug's Life

  • 1914: Born in Cresco, Iowa
  • 1933: Leaves his family's farm to attend the University of Minnesota, thanks to a Depression era program known as the "National Youth Administration"
  • 1935: Stops school to save money, works in the Civilian Conservation Corps, helping starving Americans
  • 1937: Finishes university and takes a job in the US Forestry Service
  • 1938: Marries Margret Gibson; inspired by Elvin Charles Stakman, returns to school to study plant breeding
  • 1941: Rejected from military service; works on applied science projects including waterproof glue and DDT
  • 1942: Receives a Ph.D. in Genetics and Plant Pathology
  • 1944: Moves to Mexico to head a new plant pathology program; breeds 6,000 strains of disease-resistant wheat
  • 1945: Discovers a way to grow wheat twice each season
  • 1953: Develops a high-yielding wheat strain for Mexico
  • 1962: Introduces high-yielding wheat strains to India
  • 1970: Receives the Nobel Peace Prize
  • 1983: Helps seven African countries increase maize and sorghum yields
  • 1984: Becomes a distinguished professor at Texas A&M University
  • 2005: Advocates for genetically modified crops to meet future food demands
  • 2009: Dies at the age of 95
